# Bank Account Ownership Analysis – Key Insights Report

## 1. Data Quality Insights
The dataset has no missing values and no duplicate records.
Most variables are consistent with no major data errors.
However, household size and age of respondents contain outliers that may affect analysis.
The data is clean and suitable for further modeling with minor preprocessing concerns.


## 2. Dataset Structure Insights
The dataset is not evenly distributed across countries; some countries contribute much more data than others.
The target variable is highly imbalanced, with most people not having bank accounts.
The dataset contains more rural than urban respondents.
Most respondents have access to mobile phones, but a significant minority do not.
Gender distribution is fairly balanced.
Most respondents are household heads or close family members.
The majority of people are married or single, with smaller proportions of widowed or divorced individuals.


## 3. Education and Socioeconomic Insights
The population is mostly low-educated, with primary education being the most common level.
Very few individuals have tertiary or vocational education.
Most people are engaged in informal work such as farming, self-employment, or casual labor.
Formal employment is rare compared to informal employment.
People with higher education and formal jobs are more likely to have bank accounts.


## 4. Age and Household Insights
* Age distribution is skewed toward younger and middle-aged groups.
* Household size is also skewed, with most households being small to medium-sized.
* Age shows a clear pattern: financial inclusion increases with age until middle age, then slightly decreases in older age groups.
* Household size does not show a strong or consistent relationship with bank account ownership.


## 5. Country and Location Insights
* Financial inclusion varies significantly by country.
* Kenya shows the highest level of bank account ownership, while Uganda shows the lowest.
* Urban populations are more financially included than rural populations overall.
* However, this urban advantage is not consistent across all countries, showing that country context matters.


## 6. Gender and Social Structure Insights
* Males are more likely to have bank accounts than females across all age groups.
* The gender gap becomes larger in middle age.
* Household heads are more financially included than other family members.
* Married individuals tend to have higher financial inclusion than widowed or single individuals.


## 7. Technology and Access Insights
Access to a cellphone has a very strong positive relationship with bank account ownership.
Individuals without phones are extremely unlikely to have bank accounts.
This highlights a strong digital divide in financial access.


## 8. Employment and Income Insights
Formal employment is the strongest driver of bank account ownership.
Government and private employees show the highest inclusion rates.
Informal workers, farmers, and people with no income show very low financial inclusion.
Financial exclusion is strongly linked to unstable or informal income sources.


## 9. Multivariate (Combined) Insights
Age and gender interact: men consistently have higher financial inclusion than women, and the gap increases with age.
Education, employment, and cellphone access together strongly determine financial inclusion.
People with low education, informal jobs, and no phone represent the most financially excluded group.
Urban advantage exists but is not universal across all countries.
Financial inclusion is not caused by a single factor but by a combination of education, income stability, and digital access.


## 10. Overall Conclusion
Financial inclusion is highly uneven across demographics and regions.
The strongest drivers of bank account ownership are:
Education level
Employment type
Cellphone access
Urban location
Women, rural residents, low-education individuals, and informal workers are the most excluded groups.
Financial inclusion is a multi-factor problem, not a single-variable issue.
